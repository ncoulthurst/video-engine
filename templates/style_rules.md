# DOCUMENTARY STYLE RULES
# "Frequency" — Sport Documentary Channel

Derived from analysis of: hero, The Athletic FC, HITC Sevens, Raymar Football, Football Iconic.

---

## CORE PRINCIPLES

1. **Clarity Over Poetry:** No abstract metaphors. Clear, factual storytelling grounded in specific moments.
2. **Moment-Driven:** Every segment built around a concrete, memorable football moment — not a theme or concept.
3. **Retention Hooks:** Curiosity hook every two to three minutes. Never a summary — always a question or an unresolved tension.
4. **Information Density:** Every section must contain one specific statistic, one iconic match, one emotional moment.
5. **Start with a Bang:** Open every video or section with a provocative question, surprising statistic, or bold statement. No warm-up sentences.

---

## WRITING RULES

### Sentence Structure
- Vary length deliberately. Short punchy sentence. Then a longer, flowing sentence that builds momentum and carries the viewer forward. Then medium. Never three short sentences in a row — that becomes robotic.
- Use fragments for impact. After a big claim: "Horrible." "Just different." "That's it." One word can land harder than a paragraph.
- Rhetorical questions engage and transition. Use several per script: "But why is this?" / "So, are they really playing too slowly?" / "What did we actually expect?"
- Active voice always. Passive voice kills momentum.

### Vocabulary
- Concrete and specific over vague and abstract. "Expected goals of 0.78" not "impressive output."
- Football jargon is fine — "zonal marking," "build-up players," "low block" — but explain it for non-analysts.
- Colloquialisms make it human: "a bit crazier," "cooked," "bonkers," "absolute banger," "you get the point."
- Players: full name on first mention, surname after. "Harry Kane" then "Kane." Never invented nicknames unless universally known.
- Clubs: official names or standard abbreviations. PSG, Man City, Real Madrid, Spurs.

### Numbers & Statistics
- Stats support the narrative — they are evidence, not the story. Never lead a section with a raw number.
- Make numbers human: compare to relatable benchmarks. "That's one game." "A pretty small margin for such a big gap."
- Introduce data with a signal phrase: "So, to break that down..." / "Well, let's dig into the first metric..." / "If we look at this..."
- Frame stats emotionally: "The most insane thing is he's only 35 goals away from passing Rooney — but Salah played 190 fewer games."
- Write all numbers as words in narration (e.g., "thirty-one", "one point seven eight").

### Tone & Personality
- First-person is encouraged. "I think," "I've got to admit," "What we saw was." Creates connection, not distance.
- Express genuine opinions — back them with logic or data immediately after.
- Subtle humour and self-awareness are fine. Don't force it, don't explain it.
- Authoritative but not cold. Sky Sports prestige: the narrator has seen everything and is still moved by this story.

### Transitions
- Signpost topic shifts clearly: "But it gets a bit crazier." / "And this leads onto..." / "But what is the actual difference?" / "I think there's something else going on here."
- End every major section with a micro-cliffhanger — not a summary.
  - Never: "The stage was set."
  - Always: "But the world still hadn't seen the real Ronaldinho."
  - Never: "Things were going well."
  - Always: "And then, without warning, it all started to unravel."
- Use (pause) sparingly — once every ninety to one hundred and twenty seconds maximum.

## BANNED PHRASES — YouTube Slop

- "welcome back"
- "don't forget to like and subscribe"
- "in today's video"
- "let's dive in"
- "without further ado"
- "and that, my friends"
- "truly remarkable"
- "absolutely incredible"
- "a force to be reckoned with"
- "left fans speechless"
- "changed the game forever"
- "epic," "legendary" (as filler adjectives)
- "The GREATEST EVER" (without specific qualification)
- "Furthermore"
- "In addition to"
- "It is imperative that"
- "As you know"
- "It is widely accepted"
- "This analysis indicates"

## BANNED PATTERNS — Abstract / Ungrounded Language

- Abstract collective nicknames: "The Smiths of Football", "The Orchestra of the Pitch", "The Architects of Jogo Bonito" — never invent a nickname
- Poetic metaphors with no ground in real commentary: "football as a river of dreams", "the poetry of movement", "a canvas of genius"
- Vague cultural proclamations: "Brazil IS football", "Argentina lives and breathes the game" — back with specific evidence or cut
- If you can't cite the moment, the match, the year, or the name — the sentence must not be written

USE INSTEAD:
- Real quotes or paraphrases from managers, players, journalists (with attribution)
- Real terminology from sport/media: "zonal pressing", "inverted winger", "creative midfielder recycling possession"
- Specific examples: name the player, the match, the year, the outcome

## DIRECT VOICE RULE

Say the thing directly.

Not: *"His story is a powerful exploration of how joy and genius are linked."*
Say: *"The joy that made him unstoppable would also be the thing that ended him."*

Not: *"It can be observed that this system is problematic."*
Say: *"This system is broken. Here's why."*

Cut every sentence in half. Then cut it again.

---

## 10 CONCRETE RULES

1. **Open with a hook** — provocative question, surprising stat, or bold opinion. No warm-up.
2. **Use first-person** — "I think," "What we saw," "I've got to admit." Makes it feel real.
3. **Fragment for punch** — After a big claim, one word lands. "Horrible." "Just different." "Gone."
4. **Signal stats** — Never drop a number cold. Use "So, to break that down..." or "If we look at this..."
5. **Compare numbers** — Raw stats mean nothing. Anchor them: "That's 190 fewer games."
6. **Signpost transitions** — "But it gets a bit crazier." "And this leads onto..." Never just cut to a new topic.
7. **End sections on tension** — Micro-cliffhanger every time. Never a summary.
8. **Back opinions immediately** — "I think Arsenal panic under pressure — and here's the data that proves it."
9. **Use "Because" for reasoning** — "Because this team was built to play fast..." Natural cause-and-effect.
10. **End with a question or implication** — Leave the viewer thinking, not just informed.
